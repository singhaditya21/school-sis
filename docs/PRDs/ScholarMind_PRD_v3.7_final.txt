1. Executive Summary
ScholarMind v3.7 is an AI-native education operating platform designed to run the full spectrum of institutions — from single-campus K-12 schools to multi-campus university systems, coaching networks, international school groups, and hybrid education enterprises. It unifies student, academic, financial, workforce, compliance, and intelligence workflows into a single configurable operating layer.
Version 3.7 carries forward every capability introduced in v3.0–v3.6 and adds a research-driven learner-record, privacy, AI adoption, payment orchestration, safeguarding, professional-licensure, and resilience layer.
1.1 Vision Statement
To become the education sector's definitive operating platform — the single system of record, workflow, and intelligence that institutions of any type, size, geography, or resource level use to manage people, programmes, finances, compliance, and outcomes with strong local fit, global scale, and trusted AI.
1.2 Mission
Deliver a unified, agent-first platform that adapts its interface, workflows, data model, regulatory pack, and analytics layer to the specific institution type — K-12, multi-campus chain, international school, college, university, coaching institute, or hybrid education group — while preserving a shared core for security, AI governance, interoperability, and equity architecture.

	•	Multi-Campus Hierarchy — centralised governance with campus-level autonomy; chain-level analytics with school-level control.

	•	Zero-Trust Security — defence-in-depth, FERPA/COPPA/GDPR/EU AI Act/UGC/AICTE compliant.
	•	Global Equity by Design — offline-first, SMS-native, crisis-mode, refugee intake — all capabilities from v2.0 fully retained.
Commercial Flexibility — supports per-institution, per-campus, per-student, and hybrid pricing by region and segment.

2. Problem Statement & Market Context
2.1 The Fragmented Education Technology Landscape
Today's educational institutions — whether a K-12 school chain, a university, or a coaching institute — are forced to operate across 3 to 8 disconnected software systems: a school ERP for administration, a separate LMS for learning, a standalone fee management tool, a payroll system, a CRM for admissions, and a spreadsheet for everything else. The result is systemic fragmentation: data exists in silos, decision-making is reactive, and the student record is incomplete.
For multi-campus institutions, this fragmentation is compounded across geography. A school chain with 50 campuses runs 50 separate data islands. The CEO cannot see a real-time view of attendance across the network. The CFO reconciles fee collection from 50 different spreadsheets. The Academic Director has no way to compare outcomes across campuses to identify best practices or underperformance.
For coaching institutes — one of the fastest-growing segments of the global education market, particularly in India, China, South Korea, and the Middle East — purpose-built management software is almost entirely absent. Batches are managed in WhatsApp groups. Test results are distributed via PDF. Student analytics are non-existent. Fee collection is informal.
For colleges and universities, legacy Student Information Systems (Banner, PeopleSoft, Ellucian) are monolithic, expensive, and not AI-native. Accreditation preparation consumes months of manual work. Faculty workload allocation is subjective. Academic advising is under-resourced. Course registration is a high-anxiety annual event.

2.3 Specific Gaps by Institution Type
Multi-Campus & Chain Schools
	•	No chain-level platform offers simultaneous central governance and campus-level operational autonomy.
	•	Cross-campus student transfer is a manual, week-long process in most chains.
	•	Chain-wide academic benchmarking does not exist in any current SIS product.
	•	HQ cannot push policy (grading rubrics, fee structures, curriculum maps) to campuses in real time.
International Schools
	•	Multi-currency fee management across 10+ countries requires manual reconciliation in every current platform.
	•	Expatriate student visa and immigration status tracking is entirely manual.
	•	Multi-board curriculum management (IB + IGCSE + AP + national boards simultaneously) is unsupported.
	•	Cross-country consolidated financial and academic reporting for international groups does not exist.
Colleges & Universities
	•	Course registration systems (Banner, PeopleSoft) are not AI-native; students cannot get personalised course advice.
	•	Faculty research management, grant tracking, and output reporting are managed outside the SIS entirely.
	•	Accreditation (NAAC, NBA, UGC, ABET, QAA) preparation is a manual, compliance-calendar-driven exercise.
	•	Academic advising is human-only, under-resourced, and reactive — students at risk of not graduating are identified too late.
Coaching Institutes
	•	No purpose-built management software exists for the $5.6B Indian coaching market.
	•	Batch management, test series scheduling, doubt clearing, and fee collection happen across WhatsApp, Google Sheets, and cash receipts.
	•	Student performance analytics (rank prediction, weak chapter identification, peer comparison) are not systematically available.
	•	Parent communication is informal and inconsistent.
	•	Franchise/multi-centre coaching operations have no consolidated platform.

2.6 Research-Led Improvements Required in This PRD — v3.5
Segment Priority & Wedge - the PRD should explicitly sequence go-to-market by segment instead of trying to win K-12, higher education, coaching, and international schools simultaneously on day one. Recommend: Phase A private chains/coaching groups in India and GCC; Phase B international schools and university groups; Phase C public systems.
Migration Factory - add a named migration workstream covering SIS/ERP data mapping, document import, gradebook/history conversion, identity matching, cutover tooling, and rollback playbooks. Incumbents stay sticky because switching risk is high.
Interoperability Standards - strengthen the API and data-exchange position with explicit support for OneRoster, LTI 1.3, SIF/Ed-Fi, SCIM, OpenAPI webhooks, finance connectors, and no-code integration templates.
Security & Incident Response - extend the security section with breach-notification SLAs, customer-facing audit packs, data-retention schedules, key-management posture, tenant-level logging, model-governance controls, and crisis communications workflows.
Experience Layer - sharpen the digital-front-door story with school website CMS, admissions funnel, communications, mobile app, and parent experience. This is where Finalsite and similar players create high switching value.
Proof of ROI - add quantified success benchmarks by segment: enrollment conversion lift, fee collection improvement, counselor/admin time saved, accreditation-preparation time reduced, and AI-assist utilisation targets.
Partner Ecosystem - formalise which capabilities are native, partner-led, or marketplace-led across payments, proctoring, transport, hostel, CRM, fundraising, learning tools, and government reporting.
Responsible AI Operations - add prompt-policy governance, human-review thresholds, model-routing rules, evaluation scorecards, hallucination controls, explainability, and segment-specific approval chains for fees, grades, advising, and compliance actions.
2.7 Beachhead Strategy, Segment Sequencing & Non-Goals — v3.5
Initial wedge: multi-campus and multi-brand education groups that operate across more than one institutional form (for example schools plus higher-ed, schools plus coaching, or international school groups with shared services) and therefore suffer from fragmented data, duplicated operations, and weak governance across campuses and countries.
Why this wedge first: incumbents are strongest inside single-segment silos, while ScholarMind is structurally differentiated when one group needs common finance, identity, analytics, AI policy controls, and executive command centres across heterogeneous institutions.
First 24 months - Wave 1: premium school chains, international groups, and diversified education groups with shared-service finance and admissions complexity. Wave 2: autonomous colleges and university systems that need stronger group reporting, payments, and AI operations. Wave 3: coaching and franchise networks where governance, testing, and collections become the expansion motion.
Non-goals for the first 24 months: pure LMS replacement deals, one-off brochure website projects, bespoke consumer tutoring marketplaces, and heavily customised on-prem implementations with no path to product repeatability.
2.8 Research-Upgraded Product Gaps Closed in v3.7
Version 3.7 closes the highest-priority research gaps that matter in current enterprise education buying cycles: portable learner records and employability pathways, standards conformance roadmaps, privacy-retention-model-training controls, role-based AI adoption, payment orchestration, K-12 safeguarding, professional licensure compliance, and resilience engineering.

AI adoption operating model: defines how institutions safely roll out AI by role, workflow, and risk level rather than treating governance as a static policy document.
Payments, safeguarding, licensure, and resilience: adds practical buying-center capabilities frequently requested by CFOs, school leaders, university compliance teams, and public-sector evaluators.

6. Security Architecture & Data Privacy
ScholarMind v3.5 extends the Zero-Trust security model from v2.0 to cover the significantly expanded attack surface of a multi-campus, multi-country, multi-institution-type platform. The core principle is unchanged: no user, device, campus, or network segment is trusted by default. Every access request is authenticated, authorised, and continuously validated — regardless of whether it originates from a school principal in Mumbai, a university professor in London, or a coaching student in Kota.
6.1 Multi-Tenant Security Model — Hierarchical Isolation
v3.5 introduces a three-tier tenancy model to match the institutional hierarchy:
	•	Group Tenant: the top-level security boundary for a multi-campus group or university system. Group admins see cross-campus analytics but never raw student PII from individual campuses.
	•	Campus/Institution Tenant: each campus or college is a fully isolated tenant at the data layer — schema-per-tenant, no possibility of data leakage between campuses, even within the same group.
	•	Department/Division Sub-tenant: within a university, departments have sub-tenant isolation; a faculty member in the Commerce department cannot access student records from the Engineering department.
	•	Cross-tenant data flows (for CampusAgent analytics) use aggregated, anonymised data only — raw PII never crosses tenant boundaries.

6.7 Privacy, Consent, Retention & Model-Training Controls — v3.7
Purpose limitation by default: every data object and workflow stores a permitted-use tag so institutions can prove why information was collected, where it may flow, and which downstream uses are prohibited.
No student-data advertising posture: ScholarMind does not use institutional student data for advertising, audience-building, or third-party behavioural targeting; partner data access is contractually and technically restricted to declared service purposes.
Collection and sharing consent controls: parental/guardian consent, student consent, legitimate-interest basis, or statutory basis can be configured by age band, institution type, and jurisdiction.
Retention schedule engine: each record category has a default retention window, legal-hold override, archive policy, and defensible deletion workflow; institutions can apply local policies without custom engineering.
Model-training exclusion controls: institution tenants choose whether their data can be used for tenant-specific tuning, global product improvement, or neither; the most restrictive setting is the default for minors and safeguarding data.
Deletion and revocation pack: supports subject access requests, export, correction, suppression, model-training exclusion certificates, and downstream deletion tasks across integrated systems.

Adoption metrics: measures AI feature activation, override rates, approval-cycle times, recommendation acceptance, time saved, exception rate, and policy violations by institution and user role.

9.2 Persistent Academic Identity
One of the most significant data architecture decisions in v3.5: a student's Academic Identity persists across institutions within the ScholarMind ecosystem. When a student moves from a ScholarMind-powered school to a ScholarMind-powered university or coaching institute, their core academic record — with explicit consent — can be linked. This gives receiving institutions immediate context, eliminates re-intake paperwork, and enables true longitudinal outcome analysis.
	•	Academic Identity is opt-in with granular consent: students/parents choose which data elements to share and can revoke at any time.
	•	Identity linkage is cryptographically signed: the receiving institution receives a consent token; without the token, no data transfers.
	•	Cross-institution data remains separated at the storage layer: linkage is at the application layer, not the database layer.
	•	Academic Identity is portable beyond ScholarMind: exports to SIF/Ed-Fi standard for portability to non-ScholarMind institutions.
9.3 Group Analytics Data Architecture
Group-level analytics (chain KPI dashboards, campus benchmarking, consolidated financials) require data aggregation across tenant boundaries. This is achieved through a dedicated Group Analytics Pipeline:
	•	Campus-level agents generate anonymised metric snapshots every 15 minutes — no PII in the snapshots.
	•	Snapshots flow to the Group Analytics Warehouse via encrypted, tenant-isolated channels.
	•	InsightAgent operates on the warehouse — it never has access to raw PII from individual campuses.
	•	Drill-down from group-level KPI to campus-level detail requires the user to switch into the campus tenant with the appropriate RBAC permissions.
9.4 Verifiable Learner Record & Employability Graph — v3.7
Core entities extended to include competency, skill assertion, evidence artefact, badge, credential, placement opportunity, employer, internship evaluation, and career outcome.
Every employability claim is traceable to evidence such as grade components, project reviews, attendance thresholds, internship feedback, or approved extracurricular achievement.
The learner record graph separates institution-authored facts, learner-contributed portfolio items, employer feedback, and externally issued credentials so trust levels remain explicit.

11.2 Key Assumptions
	•	Multi-campus groups have a designated IT coordinator at HQ and at each campus.
	•	Universities have an IQAC Coordinator willing to act as AccredAgent's human review partner.
	•	Coaching institutes have at least one technically proficient staff member to manage the platform.
	•	International deployments can secure legal entity and data residency compliance in each country before go-live.
	•	Faculty at higher education institutions will engage with the faculty portal for grade submission and research logging.
	•	LLM API providers (Anthropic, OpenAI) maintain their service availability and pricing SLAs.
	•	All v2.0 assumptions (connectivity, leadership commitment, biometric consent) remain valid.
11.3 Constraints
	•	Per-student / per-seat pricing must remain affordable across institution types; coaching institutes are particularly price-sensitive.
	•	University deployments may require parallel operation with legacy Banner/PeopleSoft systems during transition; migration tools required.
	•	Accreditation module requires 12–24 months of historical data before it can generate meaningful SSR narratives — institutions must plan early adoption.
	•	International deployments in new countries require 3–6 months of legal, tax, and data residency compliance work before first student record can be processed.
	•	Research module for PhD management requires careful alignment with each university's academic regulations — not fully standardisable.
